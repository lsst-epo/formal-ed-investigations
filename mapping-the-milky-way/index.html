Mapping the Milky Way

Introduction

We live inside a galaxy called the “Milky Way.” Our galaxy contains over 400 billion stars and we now have a good idea of its size and shape as well as our location within it. Our galaxy is too big to travel outside of it to take a picture. So how can we know what our galaxy looks like when we are inside it?

Imagine you lived in a house and you wanted to know what it looks like but you can’t go outside. You could get a good idea by looking around your house and seeng where and how big the rooms are. You could then look out the windows at other houses and see if any look similar to what you think your house looks like.

We can figure out what the Milky Way looks like in a similar way: by mapping out the locations of stars inside our galaxy and by comparing the shape and size of our galaxy to other galaxies.

In this investigation you will learn how to use LSST data to determine what kind of galaxy we live in. You’ll also learn how to figure out where in the Milky Way we live. We can learn both by mapping the locations of stars in the sky.

How to Map the Night Sky

LSST will measure the locations of about 17 billion stars in about a third of the entire sky. It would be impossible to display all of the stars on a computer screen, so instead we will use a data visualization that shows the concentration of stars at different locations in the sky. Concentration maps show the number of stars in each location by displaying different colors on the map. Examine the color band at the map bottom: e.g., a red color indicates a high concentration of stars whereas black indicates there are no stars at that location. White areas of the map are places that LSST has not observed.

If a location appears green, how many stars are located there?

Why are some locations white? Does this mean there are no stars at that location?

Note that the concentration map may be for different types of objects. For example, a map may show only the number of red stars at each location.

Let’s practice using this type of map. A concentration map for stars detected through the LSST y-band filter will appear in the display window of the tool. The y-band filter passes infrared light from stars. Infrared light is not blocked by dust clouds, so we can see more of the stars than those that appear only in visible light. What kind of galaxy is the Milky Way? Where Do We Live in Our Galaxy?

The distribution of stars in the sky would change depending on the type of galaxy you live in and your location in the galaxy. To understand how this works we will investigate six simulated galaxy maps, using two galaxy types and three locations in each galaxy. As you explore these scenarios, think about how they are similar and different. Specifically, keep these three questions in mind for each of the six scenarios:

  1. Is there a band of color that indicates a high concentration of stars across the map?
  2. Is there a single location on it that has the highest concentration of stars?
  3. Are there regions of the map where there are no stars at all?
